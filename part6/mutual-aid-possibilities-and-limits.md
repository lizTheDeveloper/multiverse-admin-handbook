# Mutual Aid: Possibilities and Limits

## What This Document Is About

As a facilitator, you need to understand:
- When mutual aid helps people through temporary crisis (good)
- When mutual aid enables chronic dysfunction (harmful)
- Why mutual aid cannot replace state functions
- How to navigate this during collective crisis (November 2025)

---

## The Core Distinction

### Temporarily Re-Enabling a Capable Person

**Scenario:**
- Person is fundamentally capable
- Hit temporary crisis (medical emergency, lost job, fleeing violence, emigrating)
- Needs bridge support to get stable
- Actively working on their situation
- Will recover capacity with time/resources

**What mutual aid does:**
- Provides temporary housing while they find permanent
- Shares resources while they rebuild
- Offers emotional support during crisis
- Connects them to other resources
- Helps them help themselves

**Outcome:**
- They stabilize
- They regain capacity
- They eventually contribute back (maybe to others, not necessarily to you)
- Mutual aid worked

**Example (November 2025):**
> Student needs to flee the US. You help them research visa options, connect them to resources, maybe host them for 2 weeks while they finalize plans. They're stressed but working hard on leaving. They get out. Mutual aid succeeded.

---

### Enabling a Chronically Incapable Person

**Scenario:**
- Person is not working on their situation
- Needs have no end in sight
- Not using other resources available to them
- Expects others to solve their problems
- No evidence they'll improve on their own
- May have severe untreated mental illness, addiction, or personality disorder

**What "helping" does:**
- Allows them to avoid addressing root problems
- Burns out helper
- Creates dependency
- Doesn't actually improve their situation
- Depletes community resources that could help others

**Outcome:**
- They don't improve (or get worse)
- Helper burns out
- Resentment builds
- Mutual aid fails
- Community capacity depleted

**Example:**
> Student constantly in crisis, refusing therapy, expecting community to manage their mental health, not working on job/skills/stability, demanding housing indefinitely, getting upset when boundaries are set. No plan, no progress, just demands. This is not mutual aidâ€”it's enabling.

## The Role of the State vs. Mutual Aid

### What the State Should Provide (But Often Doesn't)

**Basic survival:**
- SNAP (food assistance)
- Medicaid (healthcare)
- Housing assistance
- Disability benefits (SSI/SSDI)
- Unemployment insurance
- Mental health services
- Crisis intervention
- Addiction treatment

**Tool for accessing state resources:**
- **findhelp.org** - Search by ZIP code for available resources
- Free, nationwide database
- Direct students here to access what still exists

**Why state, not mutual aid:**
- **Scale:** Millions of people need foodâ€”mutual aid can't feed everyone
- **Expertise:** Medical care, psychiatric care require professionals
- **Consistency:** People need reliable ongoing support, not dependent on volunteer capacity
- **Rights-based:** Should be guaranteed, not charity
- **Distributed burden:** Taxes spread cost across society, not on individuals

**Mutual aid cannot replace these functions.** It's not designed to.

---

### What Mutual Aid Can Do

**Mutual aid works for:**
- **Temporary crisis support:** Bridge until state resources kick in
- **Filling gaps:** When state fails or during transitions
- **Peer support:** Emotional support, solidarity, community
- **Resource sharing:** Information, connections, small material aid
- **Collective action:** Organizing for change
- **Cultural survival:** When institutions fail marginalized people

**Mutual aid is:**
- Horizontal (peer to peer)
- Voluntary
- Limited by volunteer capacity
- Meant to complement, not replace, systemic support

## November 2025 Context: When the State Collapses

### The Current Reality

**What's happening:**
- Government shutdown
- Federal programs defunded or suspended
- SNAP, Medicaid, disability benefits uncertain
- Mental health services gutted
- People fleeing fascism/targeting
- Resource scarcity everywhere

**Result:**
- More people in crisis than ever
- Fewer state resources than ever
- Mutual aid networks overwhelmed
- Community capacity stretched to breaking

### The Impossible Bind

**Mutual aid is being asked to do what it cannot do:**
- Replace SNAP for food security
- Replace Medicaid for healthcare
- Replace housing assistance
- Replace mental health services
- Support people fleeing en masse

**This is unsustainable.**

**But people still need help. So what do we do?**

## Navigating This as a Facilitator

### Assessing Capacity and Fit

**You cannot help everyone. Your capacity is finite.**

Understanding this reality helps you make sustainable decisions about where mutual aid will actually work versus where it might enable harm.

**When mutual aid is most effective:**

1. **People experiencing temporary crisis who are actively working on their situation**
   - They have a plan and are taking steps toward stability
   - They need bridge support, not indefinite care
   - Short-term help will make a meaningful difference

2. **Community members who participate reciprocally**
   - They contribute when they can, receive when they need
   - There's genuine mutual aid (not one-directional help)
   - They're part of the fabric, not just consumers of support

3. **People accessing multiple forms of support**
   - They're applying for benefits, seeing professionals, using available resources
   - Community supplements but doesn't replace other support systems
   - They understand the community's limitations

**When community support alone isn't enough:**

Some situations indicate someone needs more intensive support than a learning community can provide:

- **Someone refusing professional help while expecting community to replace therapy/case management/crisis services** â€” This prevents them from getting what they actually need
- **Repeated boundary violations after clear communication** â€” This harms the person doing the violating AND the community
- **Chronic patterns with no movement toward change** â€” This suggests needs that require specialized intervention
- **Someone not taking steps toward their own stability** â€” Mutual aid works when people are working on their situation; it fails when it becomes enabling

**This isn't about worthiness. It's about fit and capacity.**

Not everyone is a good fit for what this space offers. That doesn't mean they're bad peopleâ€”it means they need something we can't provide. Acknowledging this protects both them (by directing them to real support) and the community (by preserving capacity for sustainable mutual aid).

**Yes, this is hard. Yes, it can feel cruel.** But pretending you can save everyone ultimately helps no one.

### Setting Limits on Mutual Aid

**You can offer:**
- Time-limited support: "I can host you for 2 weeks"
- Specific help: "I can help you research visas"
- Bounded emotional support: "I can listen for 30 minutes"
- Resource connections: "Here's where to apply for X"

**You cannot offer:**
- Indefinite housing
- All their emotional support
- Replacement for professional mental health care
- Unlimited time/energy
- Everything they need

**Say this explicitly:**
- "I can help with [specific thing] for [specific time]. After that, you'll need [other resource]."
- "I'm not a therapist. You need professional mental health support."
- "The community can't replace SNAP/Medicaid/housing assistance. You need to access those resources too."

---

### When to Stop Helping

**Stop helping when:**

1. **No progress after reasonable time**
   - You've helped for weeks/months
   - Their situation hasn't improved
   - They're not working on it

2. **Boundary violations**
   - You set limits, they ignore them
   - They guilt-trip or manipulate
   - They weaponize vulnerability

3. **You're burning out**
   - Resentment building
   - Your own stability threatened
   - You're avoiding them

4. **They refuse professional help**
   - Clear mental health crisis, refuse therapy
   - Addiction issues, refuse treatment
   - Medical issues, refuse doctor

5. **Community capacity depleted**
   - This one person is consuming all community resources
   - Others who could benefit aren't getting support

**How to stop:**
- "I've reached my limit. I can't continue helping with this."
- Connect them to professional resources
- Set hard boundary
- Follow through

**They may react badly. That's not your responsibility.**

## Recognizing Your Own Limits

### You Are Not the State

**You cannot:**
- Provide someone's entire support system
- Replace therapy
- Replace addiction treatment
- Replace housing assistance
- Guarantee anyone's survival

**Even if:**
- They have no one else
- The state has failed them
- They'll be homeless/hungry/unsafe without you
- They're begging

**You still cannot save them.**

**Trying to be the state for someone:**
- Burns you out
- Doesn't actually solve their problem
- Enables them to avoid addressing root issues
- Depletes resources that could help others

---

## The Guilt

### You Will Feel Guilty

**When you set limits, you'll think:**
- "But they really need help"
- "What if something bad happens to them?"
- "I'm abandoning them"
- "I have privilege, I should give more"

**This is normal. The guilt doesn't mean you're wrong.**

**Reality:**
- You have limits
- Exceeding them helps no one
- You cannot save everyone
- Boundaries protect your capacity to help anyone

**The state created this situation by failing people. You didn't.**

**You're a facilitator/volunteer, not a social safety net.**

## For Students Who Need More Than You Can Give

### The Conversation

**What to say:**

> "You need more support than this community can provide. We're volunteers, not professional services. You need [therapy/case management/housing assistance/addiction treatment]. Here are resources for accessing that."

**Key resource to share:**

**ðŸ” findhelp.org**
- Search by ZIP code for free resources
- Food banks, housing assistance, mental health services, healthcare
- Fastest way to find local support
- Point students here FIRST before deeper conversation

**If they push back:**

> "I understand the state has failed you. I cannot replace the state. I don't have the training, the capacity, or the resources. Accepting my limits helps both of us."

**If they weaponize vulnerability:**

> "If you're in crisis, here's 988. If you're unsafe, here's [domestic violence hotline/emergency services]. I care about your wellbeing AND I cannot be your crisis support."

**If they guilt-trip:**

> "I understand you're scared/desperate/hurting. My boundary stands."

## Mutual Aid During Collapse

### What's Different Now (November 2025)

**Normally:**
- Mutual aid fills gaps
- State provides baseline
- Community supplements

**Now:**
- State is failing
- Mutual aid is all there is for many
- Community is drowning

**This changes the calculus:**
- More people need help
- Fewer resources available
- Triage is harder
- Burnout is everywhere

**You still can't save everyone.**

**In fact, you need to be MORE careful about boundaries, not less.**

**Why:** If you burn out trying to replace the state, you help no one.

---

## Coordinating Community Resources

### Don't Let One Person Consume Everything

**Watch for:**
- One person monopolizing facilitator time
- One person consuming all community support
- Others not getting help because resources depleted

**Intervene:**
- Distribute support across multiple people
- Set per-person limits
- Triage ruthlessly
- Say no to demands that would prevent helping others

**Community sustainability > individual unlimited support**

---

## When Someone Is Chronically Incapable

### They May Need Institutional Support

**Some people need:**
- Long-term psychiatric care
- Residential addiction treatment
- Disability benefits with case management
- Conservatorship/guardianship (in extreme cases)
- Institutional support mutual aid cannot provide

**This is not failure. This is reality.**

**You cannot be:**
- Their therapist
- Their case manager
- Their guardian
- Their institution

**If someone needs this level of support:**
- Help them connect to appropriate resources
- Set boundaries on what community can offer
- Accept you cannot meet their needs

## The Difference in Action

### Example 1: Mutual Aid Success

**Student A:**
- Fleeing targeting, needs to leave US
- Researching visas, working with immigration lawyer
- Asks community for advice on Portugal vs. Thailand
- Needs temporary housing (2 weeks) while finalizing
- Emotional support during scary transition
- Respects boundaries, grateful, working hard

**Your response:**
- Connect them to emigration resources
- Offer to host for 2 weeks (if capacity)
- Share information
- Emotional support
- They leave, stabilize, maybe help next person fleeing

**Outcome:** Mutual aid worked.

---

### Example 2: Enabling Chronic Dysfunction

**Student B:**
- Constantly in crisis (every week a new emergency)
- Refusing therapy ("therapists don't understand me")
- Demanding community be 24/7 emotional support
- Asking for indefinite housing with no plan
- Violating boundaries when set
- Not working on situation
- Weaponizing mental health ("if you don't help I'll hurt myself")
- No evidence of improvement after months

**Your response:**
- Set hard boundaries
- Refuse indefinite support
- Connect to crisis resources (988, mental health services)
- Say explicitly: "You need professional help we cannot provide"
- Stop engaging if boundaries violated
- Protect community resources for others

**Outcome:** They may not get better. That's not your responsibility. You cannot save them.

## Remember

**Mutual aid is beautiful and powerful.**

**Mutual aid has limits.**

**You are not the state.**

**You cannot replace SNAP, Medicaid, housing assistance, professional mental health care.**

**During collapse, you'll be asked to do more than you can. You still have limits.**

**Helping a capable person through temporary crisis = mutual aid.**

**Boundaries protect your capacity to help anyone.**

**The state created this situation. You didn't. You can't fix it alone.**
